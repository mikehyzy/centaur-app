User Guide

How Centaur works

A reference for understanding the session flow, classification logic, dashboard, and data controls.

What Centaur does

Centaur is a cognitive instrument — not a productivity tool, not a wellness app. It does three things and nothing else:

  • Logs every AI session you complete with behavioral signals
  • Classifies each session as Direct, Delegate, or Defer based on those signals
  • Surfaces your pattern as a ratio and trend over time

It does not coach you. It does not block you. It does not tell you what Direct or Defer means for you specifically. It shows you the measurement. You draw the conclusion.

The core question Centaur answers: Is your use of AI enhancing your reasoning over time, or replacing it? The drift signal — whether your Defer rate is rising, stable, or falling relative to your baseline — is the instrument reading that answers this.

Privacy: Everything Centaur stores stays on your device. Your session data is written to your browser's local storage. Nothing is sent to any server. Clearing your browser cache will delete your history — see Your data for how to back up.

The three classifications

01
DIRECT
You held the thinking. You articulated a position before engaging with AI. You inspected the output critically. Your reasoning led — AI was absent or confirmatory.
02
Delegate
You handed a bounded outcome to AI but remained the decision authority. You defined the scope, inspected the reasoning, and directed the output. You led — AI assisted.
03
Defer.
You outsourced judgment itself. You accepted an AI output without a prior framing of your own, without challenging the reasoning, or with minimal inspection time. AI led — you accepted.

These are not judgments. None of the three classifications is good or bad. Direct is not better than Delegate. Defer is not a failure. They are measurements. A Defer on legal boilerplate is rational. A Defer on a strategic decision worth thinking through is worth knowing about.

The classifications earn their meaning from your pattern over time, not from any individual session.

How a session works

Every session follows the same three-stage flow. The order is fixed. You cannot skip stages.

1
Write your framing first
Before you can see the AI's response, you must submit your own reasoning. The field asks: "What's your take before you look at the AI's answer?" There is no minimum length requirement — a sentence is fine. A fragment counts. What matters is that something of yours exists before AI's output does.
Centaur logs: character count, time you spent on this stage.
2
Paste the AI's output and review it
Your framing stays visible above the AI output field so you can see the gap — what you expected versus what AI produced. Paste or type the AI's response in the lower field. You can select any text in the AI output to log a challenge: select the text, and a ⚑ Challenge button will appear. Click it to mark that section. Challenge counts are logged as a behavioral signal.
Centaur logs: AI output length, time from paste to submission, number of challenges marked.
3
Receive the verdict
After a brief analysis period, Centaur classifies the session as Direct, Delegate, or Defer. The verdict is one word. No explanation is given on the verdict screen — the logic is transparent in session detail, but the verdict screen is intentionally austere. You can add an optional note. You can override the verdict if it feels wrong — see Overriding a verdict. Save to return to the dashboard.
⏱
The timer is invisible
Centaur runs a timer from the moment the framing gate appears. You never see it. This is intentional — displaying a timer would change how you write your framing. The time signal is captured in the background and used in classification.
⚑
How to mark a challenge
In Stage 2, select any text in the AI output field with your cursor. A floating ⚑ Challenge button will appear near your selection. Click it to mark the section — it will be highlighted in the field. Each challenge you mark is counted. The count feeds into classification.

Reading the verdict

The verdict screen shows one word: DIRECT, Delegate, or Defer. No score, no explanation, no coaching. The verdict is shown with 2–3 seconds of analysis time before it appears — this is real computation, not theater.

Below the verdict, you will see four behavioral signals: framing time, AI review time, framing length, and challenge count. These are the raw inputs that produced the classification. You are not shown the computed score — only the inputs.

If the verdict feels wrong, you can override it. See Overriding a verdict.

How classification works

Centaur does not read your framing or AI output for content. Classification is behavioral — based on signals about how you engaged, not what you said.

Signal Direct Delegate Defer
Framing length 150+ characters 50–150 characters Under 50 characters
Framing time 2+ minutes 30s – 2 min Under 30 seconds
AI review time 90+ seconds 30–90 seconds Under 30 seconds
Word divergence High — your framing diverges substantially from AI output Medium — partial overlap Low — your framing closely mirrors AI output
Challenge count 2 or more 1 None

Each signal is scored 1–3. The five scores are summed. Total ≥ 12 = Direct. Total 8–11 = Delegate. Total ≤ 7 = Defer.

Word divergence compares the substantive words in your framing against the substantive words in the AI output. High divergence means your framing introduced ideas the AI did not — a signal of independent thinking. Low divergence means you essentially asked for what AI produced — a signal of deferral.

This heuristic is in beta. It was designed to be directionally accurate, not perfectly precise. Individual session classifications may be wrong. The pattern across sessions is more reliable than any single verdict.

The dashboard shows three things: the drift indicator (week 4+), the current week's Direct/Delegate/Defer ratio, and 4-week sparklines for each classification.

The ratio is computed from all sessions you logged during the current week. It resets weekly. If you logged no sessions this week, the ratio shows a message rather than stale numbers.

The sparklines show the trend for each classification type over the past four weeks. Each line reflects the percentage of sessions classified that way in each week.

Session count appears in the top bar: sessions this week and all time. A lower session count means the ratio is less reliable — a single session can swing percentages dramatically.

Baseline mode

During your first two weeks of use, Centaur operates in baseline mode. Your sessions are classified and stored normally, but the ratio display shows dashes instead of percentages, and the drift indicator is inactive.

This is intentional. If you saw your ratio immediately, you would optimize for it — logging sessions that improve your Direct percentage, or adjusting your behavior to hit a number. The baseline period captures your natural pattern before you've been exposed to the measurement.

After week 2, the ratio unlocks. After week 4, the drift indicator activates — because drift is computed relative to your weeks 1–2 baseline, four weeks of data are needed for a meaningful comparison.

During baseline: The dashboard shows your session count and a banner indicating how many weeks remain. Your session history is visible. The ratio and drift are hidden.

The drift signal

The drift indicator is the product's primary signal. It shows whether your Defer rate is rising, stable, or falling relative to your weeks 1–2 baseline.

A rising Defer rate means you are deferring to AI more than you did at the start. A falling Defer rate means you are deferring less. Stable means your pattern hasn't changed materially.

The drift signal is not a judgment. A rising Defer rate could reflect a change in your work (more routine tasks, a busier week) or a genuine shift in how you engage with AI. Centaur does not distinguish between these explanations — it shows the measurement and leaves interpretation to you.

How drift is computed: Centaur calculates your average Defer percentage across weeks 1–2 (your baseline). It compares each subsequent week's Defer percentage to that baseline. A difference of more than 5 points in either direction is classified as rising or falling. Within 5 points is stable.

The drift indicator shows only direction and magnitude — not a score, not a recommendation, not an explanation.

Revisiting sessions

Any session in your history can be revisited. Open the session from the History tab, read your original framing and the AI output, and select one of three actions:

  • Affirm — your original reasoning still holds. The decision or framing you had then is the one you stand behind now.
  • Refine — you'd change something. Your reasoning was on track but incomplete, or the context has shifted.
  • Reverse — you now disagree with the decision or framing. The original read was wrong.

Revisit actions are logged with a timestamp. Centaur tracks your reversal rate over time — the rate at which your original framings turn out to be wrong. A high reversal rate on Defer sessions is informative. So is a high reversal rate on Direct sessions.

Revisiting is a deliberate act. The three options are designed to have enough weight that you pause before selecting. There is no "maybe" option.

Overriding a verdict

After receiving a classification, you can override it on the verdict screen. Overrides are allowed because the behavioral heuristic is imperfect — you may have had a full reasoning process before opening AI that is not captured in your framing length, or you may have spent time reviewing AI output for reasons unrelated to inspection depth.

To override: on the verdict screen, click one of the two alternative classifications shown below the verdict. The new classification will apply when you save the session.

Override is itself a behavioral signal. The fact that you overrode a classification, and in which direction, is recorded in your session data. A pattern of overriding Defer verdicts toward Delegate or Direct — if consistent — tells you something about how the heuristic is calibrated for your work style.

Overrides appear in session history with a small asterisk (*) next to the classification label. In session detail, the original classification and the override are both shown.

Your data

All Centaur data is stored in your browser's local storage. Nothing is transmitted to any server. The tradeoff: if you clear your browser cache, your session history is gone.

To protect against this, export your data regularly.

↓
Exporting your data
In the History tab, click ↓ Export. This downloads a .json file containing all your sessions and user profile. Store this file somewhere safe. The filename includes today's date.
↑
Importing data
In the History tab, click ↑ Import. Select a previously exported .json file. Your session history will be restored. This replaces your current session data — if you have new sessions since your last export, they will be overwritten. Export first if you want to keep them.
⚙
Week simulator (dev mode)
The gear icon (⚙) in the top-right nav opens the week simulator. This lets you jump to a specific week to see what the dashboard looks like at different stages — baseline mode, first signals, drift active. It does not affect your real session data. New sessions logged while a simulated week is active are tagged to that week.

Getting the most out of it

Write your framing genuinely. The classification algorithm does not read your words for content — but you do. The value of the forced reasoning step is that it makes you articulate something before AI does. A one-sentence fragment that captures your actual take is worth more than a performative paragraph.

Log sessions consistently. Centaur's signal is a ratio. A single session means nothing. Five sessions per week for four weeks is enough to see a pattern. Two sessions per week is better than none. The baseline period (weeks 1–2) is particularly important to log honestly, since everything after is measured against it.

Log the Defer sessions too. There is a natural temptation to only log sessions where you engaged deeply. Logging Defer sessions honestly is what makes the ratio accurate. A GDPR boilerplate you handed to AI in 30 seconds is a Defer — log it.

Use revisit when something resolves. When a decision you logged plays out — when you learn whether your reasoning was right — go back and revisit the session. The reversal rate is one of Centaur's most meaningful long-term signals.

Don't over-interpret individual verdicts. The heuristic is directionally right, not precisely right. If a single verdict feels wrong, override it and move on. Watch the pattern over four weeks, not the individual data point.
